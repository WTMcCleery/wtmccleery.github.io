Research

What drives the development of an organism? How do individual cells coordinate their motion to properly form tissues and organs? D'Arcy Thompson first questioned the forces that shape tissues. His work was followed up by developmental biologists, but the emphasis was on genetics. At this moment in history, we can investigate the physics of development - building on this wealth of genetic information and take advantage of state-of-the-art microscopy and laser microsurgery. My research focuses on the mechanics and signaling of cellular coordination.

Biomechanics of Drosophila Embryogenesis

Midway through Drosophila embryogenesis, two epidermal tissues dramatically untagle in a process that requires cellular coordination spanning the entire embryo. We have found that this stage of development, called germband retraction, is mechanically robust to cell-level tension. This finding suggests that during this stage, the embryo does not require careful regulation of force-producing genes. Instead, cells coordinate their directed migration through their shape. Highly elongated cells in one tissue, the amnioserosa, are sufficient for successful retraction even with abnormal tensions among the cells. In this case, and perhaps more generally, cellular force strengths are less important than the carefully established cell shapes that direct them.

Calcium signaling in Drosophila Wound Healing

When the epidermis is injured, undamaged cells surrounding the wound must coordinate their healing effort. Cells can communicate via calcium signaling, which is actively channeled away from the wound. In the Drosophila pupal notum, we have found this signaling occurs in a series of waves expanding and flaring several cells away. This signal then coordinates cell migration to fill the wounded region.

I am modeling this process using a chemo-mechanical vertex model. Each cell in the model is represented as a 2D polygon with mechanically active components to allow migration. The mechanical forces are activated by chemical signaling among cells. Using this model, I hope to learn how cell communication coordinates cell migration.

Experimental Microenvironment Testing Plant Root Branching in Complex Soil

Unlike animals, plants remain seditary after growth. They must gather all of the nutrients for life from their local environment. The root system has developed remarkable capability to adapt to changing soil conditions and optimize nutrient collection. Strategic planning of the root architecture requires careful monitoring of the soil and coordination of cells that will develop lateral root structures. I am investigating root cell communication via hormone signaling using a novel microenvironment. This microenvironment is composed of a microfluidic device that can control nutrient delivery along the primary root axis. With spatial and temporal control of nutrients, I use fluorescence microscopy to visualize hormone response among cells. I can then model cell communication by hormone signaling and learn how roots determine when and where to branch into lateral roots.
